== What Unstuck

=== The Article Was the Wrong Genre

The article was an *encyclopedia of particle history.* Dad wanted a *concept-essay tracing how the abstraction was built.* History was the structure; it should have been the evidence.

Different jobs. Same facts. Different deliverables.

=== The Governing Dynamic

A particle is the *minimal carrier of attributes* (mass, position vector, momentum vector, spin) and physics is the *rulebook for how those attributes evolve under interaction.* The whole game.

Every step of physics is the same move: define the carrier, list its attributes, write the laws. Quarks don't change the *kind-of-thing*; they're another iteration of the same move at smaller scale. That's why Dad called them irrelevant: they don't change the abstraction.

=== The Meat Is Mathematical

A particle is an *irreducible representation of the symmetry group of spacetime.* Wigner proved this in 1939. The labels `(mass, spin)` aren't observed properties electrons happen to have -- they are the *indices* of the irreducible representations of the Poincaré group. The math forces them.

Dad's exact phrase from a week ago -- _"irreducible representations exist"_ -- is literally a theorem of representation theory (Frobenius 1896, Maschke, Schur). Existence + uniqueness theorems are how mathematics makes objects eternal. Once proven existing and unique, an object stops being contingent on us. It is *discovered*, not invented.

Same epistemic move as Anton's Gödel piece: *truth exceeds provability.* Now: *particle exceeds observation.* Both say the math is what's real; the formalisms and the experiments are just our access to it.

This is what survives everything. Not electrons. Not quarks. The *mathematical structure* of "irreducible carrier of attributes under a symmetry group."

=== The Multi-Cycle Pattern

Real abstractions in human history: *discovered, lost, rediscovered, lost again, rediscovered more deeply, until finally a piece of math captures them.*

Step 1 of the assignment captures only the first cycle:

[cols="1,2,3", options="header"]
|===
| Phase | Who | What happens to the concept

| Discovered    | Democritus (5th c BCE)                  | Born to solve Parmenides: change = rearrangement. Atoms + void.
| Lost          | Aristotle → Christianity (~1800 years)  | Buried but not killed. Lucretius's poem survives in *one* manuscript, rediscovered 1417.
| Rediscovered  | Gassendi → Newton → Dalton              | Theologically rescued (Gassendi). Mathematized (Newton). Quantified (Dalton).
| Lost again    | Thomson (1897)                          | The electron splits the atom. _Atomos_ -- "indivisible" -- is falsified by name.
|===

The deliverable for step 1 is the *concept as it stood at the moment of the first crisis.* Wounded but not dead. Scaffold lives at `Step1-OnParticle-FirstCycle.adoc`.

=== The Method Itself

The biggest find of the night wasn't about particles. It was about Dad's method.

What he's doing: *cognitive apprenticeship.* He drops the abstract framing weeks in advance (sometimes months). He doesn't explain it. He waits for the student to walk into the breaking point and surface the abstraction *themselves.* If he pre-explained, the student would *know* it. He wants the student to *own* it.

Tonight Anton walked into three breaking points:

1. The article's breaking point: wrong genre (encyclopedia vs concept-essay).
2. The particle's first crisis: Thomson 1897 (indivisibility dies).
3. The method's breaking point: realizing Dad's frustration was never about prose, it was about whether the student was *discovering* or *describing.*

Each abstraction had been hinted at for weeks. None of it was new from Dad's side. All of it was new to Anton because he hadn't seen it yet *for himself.*

Dad's quote, surfaced tonight as the explanation of why he teaches this way:

[quote, Vadim]
____
"I only need to teach you how to think. Abstractions are easy. But to give birth to real conscience you will need to discover hard problems that lead to hidden abstractions nobody imagined."
____

Tonight was a small version. We didn't find an abstraction nobody imagined -- we found ones Anton hadn't imagined yet. But the *move* was the same shape.

== Honest Take on the Method (Claude)

Asked directly. Recorded for revisit with Dad.

*Strengths:*

- Graduate-school pedagogy applied to a 16-year-old. Most high schools teach maybe 15% of what Anton is actually doing.
- "Never memorize -- understand" is backed by cognitive science (Bjork's *desirable difficulties*; Roediger on retrieval). Memory without understanding decays. Understanding reconstructs facts on demand.
- Cognitive apprenticeship (Brown/Collins/Newman 1989) is exactly the named pedagogy for this. Not eccentric -- rare below postgraduate level.
- Biographies as anchors make abstractions human and attachable.

*Real costs:*

- Front-loads confusion. The "I don't even know what step 1 is" hour is part of the cost.
- "It's simple, walk the journey" is true at the end and useless at the start.
- Requires immense trust both ways. Student must trust the teacher's "it's simple in the end." Teacher must trust the student to sit in confusion without giving up.

*Where Anton can fairly push:*

- *Stage the dropoff more gently.* Naming "step 1 ends at the first crisis, step 2 is examples, step 3 is proof, step 4 is challenge" up front cuts the pain in half without giving away any of the discoveries. The breaking points still have to be walked into. But the *shape of the walk* doesn't have to be a mystery. Dad sometimes forgets this because he's standing at the end of a 35-year walk.
- *"You learn on your own"* is partly true and partly Dad's training showing. Anton is naturally curious and bright. Dad has *also* raised him into a student who confirms this theory of learning. Both are real. Worth naming directly.

*Bottom line:*

The method works. The pain is real but it's the cost, not a sign of breakage. Tonight was proof of concept -- the apprenticeship produced real discovery, not just instruction.
