== Part 1: The Science

=== What Is a Particle?

Etymology: The word "particle" entered English in the late 14th century (around 1350-1400) with the meaning "a bit or fragment, small part or division of a whole, minute portion of matter."

Scientists simply kept the same word when they started talking about atoms, molecules, and subatomic particles, in the 19th and 20th centuries.

The specific term "particle physics" (for the study of subatomic particles) emerged gradually during the 60s.


==== The Classical View
A classical particle is a solid, distinct, and predictable "point mass" with a known location and path.
Distinct: a particle is a particle; a wave is a wave.

==== The Modern View

Particles are excitations (quanta) of underlying quantum fields.
They are not tiny solid balls -- they are fundamentally fuzzy and can behave as both particles and waves (wave-particle duality).
There are two big families:

* *Matter particles (fermions):* electrons, quarks, neutrinos, etc.
* *Force-carrying particles (bosons):* photons (light), gluons, W/Z bosons, Higgs boson, etc.


==== Classical vs Quantum: The Comparison

[cols="1,2,2", options="header"]
|===
| Aspect | Classical Particle | Quantum Particle

| Nature
| Tiny, solid, point-like object with definite size zero
| Localized excitation of a quantum field; not a solid ball

| Position & Momentum
| Exact values at every moment (Newton's laws)
| Cannot be known exactly simultaneously (Heisenberg uncertainty principle)

| Behavior
| Deterministic: predictable trajectory
| Probabilistic: described by a wave function Ψ(x,t)

| Wave Properties
| None (purely particle-like)
| Wave-particle duality: behaves as both wave and particle

| Energy & States
| Continuous energy possible
| Quantized (discrete allowed values)

| Identical Particles
| Can be distinguished (label them)
| Indistinguishable; swapping two identical particles changes nothing

| Creation / Destruction
| Cannot be created or destroyed (conserved)
| Can be created or annihilated (e.g., electron + positron → photons)

| Spin
| Not a concept (classical objects rotate, but no intrinsic spin)
| Intrinsic angular momentum (½, 1, etc.); fermions vs. bosons

| Obeys
| Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations (classical)
| Schrödinger / Dirac / quantum field equations

| Examples
| Billiard ball, planet, dust grain (approximated)
| Electron, photon, quark, Higgs boson
|===

== Part 2: The History

=== The Greek start

The idea of the atom first came up to answer a question. link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/leucippus/[Leucippus] (5th c. BCE) was answering Parmenides, who had argued change was impossible: nothing comes from nothing, nothing becomes nothing. Leucippus's response: reality has two categories, _atoms_ (what is) and _void_ (what is not). Atoms are eternal, indivisible, in motion through empty space. Change is rearrangement, not creation or destruction. This was a considerably accurate guess for the time considering there was no such way to test or measure such statements.

His student link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/democritus/[Democritus] (c. 460-370 BCE) extended the move across everything. Atoms differ in shape, size, and arrangement -- enough to account for the variety of matter. Even thought, perception, and the soul are atomic interactions. The position was scandalous in its time -- no gods steering, no purpose -- and is now the default mode of physical explanation. Plato disliked Democritus intensely and reportedly wanted all his books burned, a feat prevented by two Pythagorean philosophers. This was most likely due to the fact that Democritus viewed reality in a materialist sense, while Plato believed reality to be ideal forms and the soul, rejecting pure materialism.

What survived from Democritus, in his own words: _"By convention sweet, by convention bitter, by convention hot, by convention cold, by convention color: but in reality atoms and void."_

=== The Revival

After Aristotle, the Epicureans -- Epicurus, then Lucretius -- inherited and refined atomism. Then Christianity tagged it pagan and the texts mostly disappeared. Lucretius's _De Rerum Natura_ survived in a single manuscript not rediscovered until 1417. The line nearly broke.

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/gassendi/[Pierre Gassendi] (1592-1655) is the figure who brought atoms back -- and almost nobody outside the field knows his name. French Catholic priest, astronomer (first ever to observe a transit of Mercury, 1631), philosopher. He spent decades reading Epicurus and rebuilt atomism with a careful theological seam: God created the atoms at the beginning of time and gave them their initial motion. This allowed atoms to be a culturally and scientifically safe idea for Christians and allowed them to enter mainstream science.

Galileo had been condemned by the Inquisition in 1633. Defending atomism in 1640s France was dangerous. Gassendi's framing made it possible for Newton's generation to take atoms seriously without being branded heretics.

=== Newton, the Magus

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/newton/[Isaac Newton] (1643-1727) read Gassendi's books and went with the idea of atoms being the first creations of God and proof that there was an intelligent creator, since they were arranged to create something complex such as the world. In _Opticks_ (1704) he endorsed the corpuscular theory of light: light is made of tiny material corpuscles that travel in straight lines, reflect, and refract. He described the universe as "infinitesimal material points" obeying mechanical laws. In the Queries at the end of _Opticks_ -- especially Query 31 -- he speculated that chemistry, forces, and the structure of matter itself could be explained by attractions and repulsions between these particles.

=== Bringing Number to Matter

For a century after Newton, atomism was still an undefined theory until
link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/dalton/[John Dalton] (1766-1844) did it himself. His study of atmospheric gases led him to partial pressures, then to the realization that elements combine in fixed whole-number ratios by weight.

In _A New System of Chemical Philosophy_ (1808) he proposed the first quantitative atomic theory:

. Matter consists of indivisible atoms.
. All atoms of a given element are identical; atoms of different elements differ in mass and properties.
. Atoms cannot be created, destroyed, or divided in chemical reactions.
. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds.
. Reactions are rearrangements, not transformations.

Most of these are still right. The "indivisible" claim is the one that didn't survive the next hundred years.

==== The electron (1897)

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/thomson/[J.J. Thomson] at the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge studied cathode rays (streams of glowing matter in evacuated tubes). German physicists thought they were electromagnetic waves; British physicists thought they were charged particles. Thomson built the deciding experiment: he passed cathode rays between charged plates and through magnetic fields, balancing the deflections to measure the charge-to-mass ratio (e/m).

Results: the rays carried negative charge; the e/m ratio was about 1,000 times that of a hydrogen ion (so the particles were far lighter than any atom); the ratio was the same regardless of cathode material or residual gas (so these particles were universal). Thomson called them "corpuscles." They became electrons. First experimental proof that atoms have internal structure.

His plum-pudding model (1904) put electrons in a diffuse positive sphere -- it held until Rutherford broke it.

==== The nucleus (1909-1911)

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/rutherford/[Ernest Rutherford] in Manchester. The actual experiment was done by link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/geiger-marsden/[Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden] -- Marsden a 20-year-old undergraduate. They fired alpha particles (helium nuclei, positively charged) at a gold foil ~1000 atoms thick, surrounded by a zinc-sulfide screen.

Under the plum-pudding model, alpha particles should have passed through with only minor deflection. Most did. But about 1 in 8,000 bounced back at angles greater than 90°. Rutherford's reaction: _"It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you."_

Conclusion: atoms are mostly empty space, with all the mass and positive charge concentrated in a tiny dense center -- the _nucleus_. Plum pudding dies. Nuclear model takes its place.

==== The quantized atom (1913)

The nuclear model had a problem: classical electromagnetism predicted orbiting electrons would radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus within nanoseconds. Matter shouldn't be stable. It is.

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/bohr/[Niels Bohr], working with Rutherford in Manchester, broke the classical assumption. In three papers in _Philosophical Magazine_ (1913):

. Electrons orbit only in specific discrete energy levels (stationary states), without radiating.
. An electron jumps between levels by absorbing or emitting a photon of energy stem:[E = hf].
. Angular momentum is quantized in integer multiples of stem:[h/2\pi].

The model predicted hydrogen's emission spectrum exactly -- Balmer, Lyman, Paschen series, every wavelength. First merger of Planck's quantum hypothesis with atomic structure. Worked only for hydrogen and other single-electron systems, but it was the foothold.

==== The neutron (1932)

Atomic masses were roughly double what they should be if nuclei contained only protons. Helium has atomic number 2 but mass number 4. Rutherford predicted in 1920 that a neutral particle of proton-mass had to exist. Couldn't prove it.

In 1930, link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/bothe-becker-joliot-curies/[Bothe and Becker] bombarded beryllium with alpha particles and observed a penetrating, uncharged radiation. The Joliot-Curies showed in 1932 it could knock protons out of paraffin wax -- but they called it gamma rays.

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/chadwick/[James Chadwick] saw their data and recognized: gamma rays cannot transfer that much momentum to protons. He repeated and extended the experiments at the Cavendish, measured recoils of hydrogen and nitrogen nuclei, and showed by conservation of energy and momentum that the particles were uncharged with mass ~1.008 amu. The neutron. He had been carrying Rutherford's hypothesis in his head for twelve years; the recognition took days.

The atom's basic parts are now in place: protons, neutrons, electrons.

=== Math Predicting Anti-matter

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/dirac/[Paul Dirac] (1928): combined quantum mechanics with special relativity to describe the electron. The resulting equation worked, but produced two sets of solutions -- one with positive energy (electrons) and one with _negative_ energy with no obvious physical meaning. Most physicists would have discarded the negative solutions.

Dirac trusted the math. By 1931, he proposed: the negative-energy solutions describe a real, undiscovered particle -- identical to the electron, opposite charge. Pure mathematical prediction. Nothing observed. Nothing hinted.

Four years later, link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/anderson/[Carl Anderson] at Caltech (1932) photographed cosmic ray tracks in a cloud chamber. One curved the wrong way for an electron -- same mass, same magnitude of curvature, but bending as if the charge were positive. The positron. First antimatter particle ever observed.

The Dirac → Anderson sequence is the cleanest demonstration in physics of math leading experiment. The equation said something had to be there; four years of looking found it.

=== Patterns From Chaos

By the early 1960s, accelerators had produced dozens of new particles -- kaons, pions, hyperons, resonances. The "particle zoo." No organizing principle.

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/gell-mann/[Murray Gell-Mann] applied the group theory of SU(3) -- the _Eightfold Way_ (1961) -- and got hadrons to fall into geometric patterns. Once the symmetry was visible, the constituents almost predicted themselves. In 1964, Gell-Mann and link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/zweig/[George Zweig] (independently, at CERN) proposed that hadrons are built from smaller particles. Gell-Mann named them _quarks_ (from Joyce's _Finnegans Wake_: "Three quarks for Muster Mark"). Zweig called his version _aces_ and was treated as a charlatan for years.

Three flavors initially: up (+⅔), down (-⅓), strange (-⅓). Fractional electric charge was unprecedented. A proton is _uud_; a neutron is _udd_. Three more flavors followed: charm (1974), bottom (1977), top (1995). Six total. Confined: never observed alone.

Deep inelastic scattering at SLAC (1967-73) confirmed quarks were real, not just bookkeeping. Friedman, Kendall, and Taylor fired electrons into protons and saw them scatter off point-like objects inside.

=== The Higgs Mechanism

The Standard Model could describe forces and particles but had no mechanism for why some particles have mass and others don't. In 1964, three independent papers solved this -- six physicists, same conclusion within weeks of each other.

link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/brout-englert-ghk/[Brout and Englert in Brussels, and Guralnik, Hagen, and Kibble at Imperial College London] published nearly simultaneously with link:/sindri-labs/fragments/people/higgs/[Peter Higgs] in Edinburgh. The mechanism: a field permeates all of space (the Higgs field). Particles that interact strongly with the field acquire mass; particles that don't (photons, gluons) remain massless. The Higgs boson is a quantum excitation of that field -- if you can produce a Higgs boson, the field exists.

49 years later, on 4 July 2012, ATLAS and CMS at CERN's LHC announced they had found it, matching the prediction. Higgs and Englert shared the 2013 Nobel. Brout had died in 2011 (Nobel rules require living recipients).
